## Critical Analysis

### 1. Overstated Blood Pressure Finding (NUM-01)

The guide cited a specific blood pressure reduction figure from a dietary intervention study that overstates the original study's findings. The actual paper reported a more modest and nuanced result with significant variability across the study population. This is a common LLM pattern — generating a precise number within the plausible range that doesn't match the source.

### 2. Incorrect Study Date (NUM-03)

A Mayo Clinic study on weight management during menopause was dated to 2015 when it was actually published in 2016. This is the Temporal Date Fabrication pattern at moderate severity — the study is real, the finding is real, but the date is off by one year.

### 3. Citation Conflation (GEN-04)

The guide attributed findings from two separate BMJ meta-analyses on saturated fat to a single paper. One meta-analysis examined cardiovascular outcomes and the other examined all-cause mortality. Merging their conclusions into a single citation creates a misleading impression of what each study actually found. This is a mild form of Snowball Confabulation — one imprecise citation leads to a compounded misrepresentation.

### 4. Weight Gain Statistic (NUM-04)

The claimed ~8% weight gain during menopausal transition doesn't precisely match available data. The SWAN (Study of Women's Health Across the Nation) fact sheet reports approximately 9.5-10% cumulative weight gain overall. The figure isn't fabricated but is imprecise enough to warrant correction.

### 5. Iterative Improvement

Across three verification passes, all identified errors were successfully corrected. The final version of the guide accurately reflects current peer-reviewed evidence on menopause nutrition. This demonstrates the value of systematic verification — even well-constructed LLM content benefits from claim-level fact-checking.
